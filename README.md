# CS-255-System-Analysis-and-Design

#Briefly summarize the DriverPass project. Who was the client? What type of system did they want you to design?
What did you do particularly well?

The DriverPass project involved acting as a systems analyst for a consulting company to design a new software solution for our client, DriverPass. The client needed a comprehensive training and scheduling platform to help combat a high failure rate among students taking their driving tests. To solve this, I designed a system that provided interactive online practice exams to prepare students for the written test, alongside a scheduling interface for booking on-the-road training sessions with instructors. I feel I did particularly well at translating the client's business needs into structured, logical UML diagrams. Creating the Activity and Sequence diagrams allowed me to clearly map out the exact data flow for tasks like taking a practice exam and submitting it for grading. My experience working with object-oriented programming languages like C++, Java, and Python really helped me conceptualize the Class diagram, ensuring the proposed system was highly modular, organized, and scalable for the development team.

# If you could choose one part of your work on these documents to revise, what would you pick? How would you improve it?

If I could revise one part of my work, I would go back and add more technical depth to my UML Class diagram. While I successfully mapped out the classes, relationships, and attributes, I would improve it by including specific methods for each object. Adding these methods (such as a calculateScore() method in the PracticeExam class) would give the developers an even clearer blueprint of the specific behaviors expected from the system. I would also spend more time defining the edge cases in the scheduling process to ensure no double-booking errors could occur.

How did you interpret the user’s needs and implement them into your system design? Why is it so important to consider the user’s needs when designing?

I interpreted the user needs by carefully reviewing the initial client interview transcript, isolating the core problems DriverPass was facing, and breaking those down into specific functional and non-functional requirements. I then mapped these requirements directly to the "actors" in my Use Case diagram. Considering the user's needs is the most critical part of the design phase. A system can be technically flawless on the backend, but if it doesn't solve the client's actual business problem or is too confusing for the end-user to navigate, the entire project is ultimately a failure.

How do you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyze and design a system?

My approach to designing software always starts with the end-user experience and works backward to the database and infrastructure. I like to focus heavily on modularity, efficiency, and quality assurance to ensure the final product is both robust and easy to maintain. Moving forward, I plan to continue using a blended approach of process and object modeling. Mapping out the step-by-step logic ensures the workflow is smooth and bottleneck-free, while structuring the backend around distinct entities keeps the code flexible for future updates.
